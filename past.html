The Past of Irish Dancing



Origin

The exact origin of Irish Dancing is difficult to determine unfortunatel. It has been theorized that Irish Dancing was first present in history in the form of religious rituals conducted by either pagan, celtic, and/or druidic individuals. During this time hundreds and hundreds of years ago, this form of dancing consisted of circular motions danced around a tree. This dance was done for everyone to see at a festival once a year called "Aonach". With the many years since then, Irish Dance jas been influenced,changed, and adjusted giving us what we now have today.

Development

As we take a look at the history of Ireland's invaders, we can also take a look at some of the influence Irish Dancing has had over history.

With the 9th century,Ireland was invaded by Vikings. With their destruction, the vikings also brought a heavy focus on technique and rhythm in dance. We can still see this being displayed in the Irish dancing of today. With the 12th century, Normans from Normandy, France invaded Ireland. It was a common tradition for a dance called 'The Carol' to be performed over conquered towns. This involved an individual who was either singing or dancing in the middle of people dancing in a circular motion. This caused irish Dancing to pick up this style of dance as well as having a new focus on dancing in line formations as well as the circular formations. British Rule

British rule of Ireland lasted from the 16th century to the 20th centruy. It was an unfortunately long time of opression that resulted in the Potato Famien, an extremely impactful event in Irish history. Thankfully, Irish individuals did not give up. They kept fighting for what they believed in, as well as fighting for their culture. We can see this fight influence Irish Dancing greatly.

One of the ways british rule was shown, was through supressing Gaelic gatherings and expressions of Gaelic culture. To fight this, Irish Dancing adopted the technique of keeping the upper body (torso,arms,head, and neck) still, while the lower half of the body danced. An Irish dancer dacning behind an open barn door (door seperated in two, top could be open while the bottom was closed) or seen through a window, would be able to hide that they were dancing. To an unsespecting individual, it would look like they were hoping or jumping ever so slightly. It was the perfect way to hide the preservation of Gaelic culture.

Dance Masters

As the outlawing of Gaelic gatherings and the diminshing of Gaelic culture relaxed a bit during the 18th century, dance masters emerged. Irish dancing had become more disciplined, leading to the mergence of dance masters. These individuals were highly skilled in irish Dancing and heald with a very high regard because of this. They would travel from town to town sharing their expertise. This helped solidy a standard of dance across multiple towns and countrysides. The emergence of dance masters helped standardize specific aspects of Irish Dance (music, technique, dance steps) across larger regions.

When Irish Dancing students wanted to show off what they had learned, performanced called "cellis" (kell-ees) were held. It was a way for every pupil to show off their skills while dancing together. Typically the best student, the soloist, would be given the chance to show off thewir exemplerary skills by themselves. As the dance mastsers had been working just as hard as the students, they soon become proud of their work and wanted to show off the skills of their students. Dance masters of other regions felt very similar, which caused dancing competitions to soon emerge.

The Gaelic League

In the late 19th century, the Gaelic League formed, to continue to preserve Irish culture after British involvement. This led to an even wider standardization of steps, music, and posture. The Gaelic League eventually led to the creation of An Coimisiun Le Rinci Gaelacha (CLRG), a governing body which standardized how Irish Dancing was taught even more, even standardizing competitions (feiseanna) and teaching certifications. When CLRG was formed in 1930, we were closer to what Irish dancing has become today.


As Irish individuals left Ireland throughout history, they took Irish Dancing with them. Wherever Irish communities settled, they brought Irish Dancing with them, making it known in America, Australia, England, and so many other countries throughout the world.
